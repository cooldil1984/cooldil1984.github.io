In 2017, I enrolled in the joint Executive MBA program between Kellogg School of Management in Chicago and WHU – Otto Beisheim School of Management in Germany. Over two intensive years, I studied alongside executives from more than 30 countries while simultaneously managing a complex global technology program.

It was, without question, the most challenging and most formative professional experience of my career.

**Why I Did It — And What I Expected**

I went into the program looking for frameworks. I expected to learn finance, strategy, marketing — the structured tools of business leadership. And I did learn those things. The coursework was rigorous and immediately applicable.

But the deeper learning came from an unexpected source: the people in the room.

**The Education of Perspective**

In one strategy workshop, a classmate who ran manufacturing operations in Southeast Asia described a supply chain challenge that, on the surface, looked nothing like my IT transformation work in Munich. As we worked through it together, I realized the underlying dynamics were identical — siloed incentives, misaligned stakeholder expectations, a reluctance to surface bad news early. The problem was universal. Only the vocabulary was different.

This happened repeatedly. A healthcare executive from Brazil. A private equity partner from London. A family business owner from the Middle East. Each conversation stripped away the surface-level specifics of their industry and revealed the timeless human dynamics underneath: trust, incentive, communication, leadership.

**The Discipline of the Dual Context**

Completing an EMBA while working full-time in a demanding role is an exercise in ruthless prioritization. There were weekends of intensive residencies, late nights balancing coursework with program deliverables, and more than a few flights between Munich and Chicago. It taught me something that no classroom exercise could replicate: how to perform at a high level under sustained pressure while continuing to invest in long-term growth.

**What It Actually Changed**

I came out of the program with sharper financial acumen, better negotiation instincts, and a genuine global network. But the most lasting change was subtler: I became more curious about the perspectives of the people across the table from me. I became a better listener.

In technology leadership, we often valorize the person who has the answer. The EMBA taught me to equally value the person who asks the right question. That shift has made every program I've led since more effective, and every team I've worked with more engaged.
