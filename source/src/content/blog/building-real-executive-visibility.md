I have sat through hundreds of program status reviews over the course of my career. And I've noticed a recurring pattern: the moment the dashboard goes green, the room relaxes. People stop asking hard questions. Decisions get deferred. And two weeks later, everyone is blindsided by a crisis that was hiding in plain sight.

Most IT dashboards are not built to inform decisions. They are built to manage upward — to provide a defensible record of "we reported the status." That's a fundamentally different purpose, and it produces fundamentally different information.

**The Problem with RAG Status**

The Red-Amber-Green model is intuitive and fast to communicate. It is also deeply susceptible to gaming. When the definition of "green" is left to the team delivering the work, there is an inherent incentive to stay green as long as possible and flip to red only when the situation is undeniable. By that point, the window for preventive action has usually closed.

I've seen programs maintain amber status for six consecutive weeks before a critical delivery failure. In retrospect, the signals were all there — velocity trends, dependency slippages, resource utilization patterns. They just weren't surfaced in a way that prompted intervention.

**What Good Executive Visibility Looks Like**

Real executive visibility is predictive, not historical. It surfaces leading indicators — the upstream signals that precede downstream problems. Things like: sprint velocity trends over the past four increments, not just the current one. Dependency health across teams, not just within them. Budget burn rate against milestone achievement, not just against timeline.

In one major program, I built an integrated portfolio dashboard combining Jira delivery data with financial actuals and risk registers. The result was a single view that allowed senior leadership to identify emerging risks two to three weeks before they would have manifested as delivery failures. That early warning translated directly into proactive decisions and, ultimately, a cleaner delivery record.

**The Design Principle**

The best dashboard I ever built was one that made it harder to stay comfortable. It was designed to surface discomfort early — to prompt the right questions before the answers became expensive. If your portfolio review consistently ends with everyone feeling reassured, your dashboard is probably not doing its job.

Build for decision-making, not for reporting. The difference shows up in outcomes.
